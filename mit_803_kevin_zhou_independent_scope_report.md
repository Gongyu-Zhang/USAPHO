## 1. Main Conclusion

The old MIT 8.03SC Fall 2012 course is a strong resource for oscillations, waves, electromagnetic waves, and optics. However, it is not designed specifically for USAPhO preparation. It often develops useful olympiad ideas in a more formal, more mathematical, or more application-heavy way than is necessary.

Using Kevin Zhou’s handouts as the guide, the course should be divided into three categories:

1. **Core and high-yield:** Study carefully.
2. **Useful but selective:** Learn the main physics ideas, but skip detailed derivations or specialized examples.
3. **Mostly beyond efficient USAPhO preparation:** Treat as enrichment unless already strong on core problems.

The most useful MIT 8.03SC content includes:

- simple harmonic motion;
- damped and driven oscillators;
- resonance;
- quality factor;
- coupled oscillators;
- normal modes;
- wave equation;
- traveling and standing waves;
- sound waves and harmonics;
- Fourier series for normal modes;
- basic wave packets;
- phase velocity and group velocity;
- electromagnetic waves;
- \(E=cB\);
- Poynting vector;
- radiation pressure;
- Doppler effect;
- polarization basics;
- Snell’s law;
- total internal reflection;
- Brewster angle;
- interference;
- thin films;
- diffraction gratings;
- single-slit diffraction;
- diffraction-limited resolution.

The most clearly beyond-scope or low-yield MIT 8.03SC content includes:

- full Fourier transform and power-spectrum formalism;
- detailed signal-processing treatment;
- heavy vector-calculus derivations of electromagnetic waves;
- arbitrary-dimensional wave equations;
- plate vibrations and biharmonic equations;
- detailed Larmor radiation derivations;
- antenna theory;
- full Rayleigh/Thomson scattering cross-section derivations;
- skin depth;
- transmission lines;
- coaxial cables;
- waveguides;
- resonant cavities;
- full Fresnel coefficient algebra;
- evanescent-wave formalism beyond basic total internal reflection;
- advanced birefringence devices;
- Faraday rotators;
- detailed water-wave theory;
- detailed rainbow, halo, and glory optics;
- black-hole, cosmology, and gravitational-wave-style enrichment.

## 2. How to Interpret Kevin Zhou’s Handout Scope

Kevin Zhou’s handouts are broader than the minimal competition syllabus. They include material useful for USAPhO, IPhO-style preparation, advanced enrichment, and sometimes material that connects olympiad physics to undergraduate-level theory.

Therefore, the presence of a topic in Kevin Zhou’s handouts does not automatically mean that every detail of that topic is high priority.

For this report, the practical interpretation is:

### High priority

A topic is high priority if it appears in Kevin Zhou’s core oscillation/wave/EM/optics material and is also commonly useful in problem solving.

Examples:

- damped and driven oscillators;
- normal modes;
- wave equation;
- standing waves;
- Fourier series;
- interference;
- diffraction;
- basic electromagnetic waves;
- Poynting vector;
- radiation pressure;
- Snell’s law;
- polarization basics.

### Medium priority

A topic is medium priority if Kevin Zhou covers it and it can appear in olympiad-style problems, but the MIT course treats it more deeply than necessary.

Examples:

- group velocity;
- wave packets;
- dispersion;
- qualitative scattering;
- Doppler effect;
- Brewster angle;
- basic conductor reflection;
- basic material-interface optics.

### Low priority

A topic is low priority if it is mainly an advanced application, undergraduate extension, or specialized physical system.

Examples:

- waveguides;
- resonant cavities;
- skin depth;
- transmission lines;
- full Fresnel equations;
- antenna emission;
- detailed rainbow/halo/glory optics;
- astrophysical applications.

## 3. Kevin Zhou Handout Mapping

### 3.1 M4 Oscillations

Kevin Zhou’s **M4 Oscillations** is the main reference for MIT 8.03SC Lectures 1–6.

It supports the study of:

- simple harmonic motion;
- small oscillations;
- Taylor expansion near equilibrium;
- damped oscillations;
- driven oscillations;
- resonance;
- transient behavior;
- steady-state response;
- phase response;
- amplitude response;
- quality factor;
- coupled oscillators;
- normal modes.

Relevant MIT lectures:

- Lecture 1: Periodic Phenomena
- Lecture 2: Damped Free Oscillations
- Lecture 3: Forced Oscillations with Damping
- Lecture 4: Resonance and Transient Phenomena
- Lecture 5: Coupled Oscillators
- Lecture 6: Driven Coupled Oscillators

#### What to learn from MIT 8.03SC using M4

Learn carefully:

- SHM equations;
- energy in SHM;
- damping regimes;
- exponential decay;
- driven oscillator steady state;
- phase lag;
- resonance;
- \(Q\) factor;
- beats;
- two-oscillator normal modes;
- symmetry methods for normal modes.

Learn selectively:

- long transient-solution algebra;
- driven coupled oscillators with many variables;
- arbitrary many-body normal-mode systems.

Mostly skip for efficient USAPhO preparation:

- large normal-mode systems requiring computation;
- advanced Hamiltonian-style interpretations;
- adiabatic invariant material unless already doing advanced enrichment.

## 8. Recommended Study Path

### Stage 1: Oscillations

Study MIT Lectures 1–5 carefully.

Use Kevin Zhou:

- M4 Oscillations

Main goals:

- solve damped oscillator problems;
- solve driven oscillator problems;
- understand resonance and \(Q\);
- find normal modes of simple systems;
- use symmetry in coupled-oscillator problems.

Lecture 6 is useful but should be studied selectively.

---

### Stage 2: Core Waves

Study MIT Lectures 7–9 carefully.

Use Kevin Zhou:

- W1 Waves I
- W3 for sound-specific topics

Main goals:

- derive and use the wave equation;
- work with traveling waves;
- work with standing waves;
- apply boundary conditions;
- solve string and sound harmonic problems.

---

### Stage 3: Fourier and Dispersion

Study selected parts of MIT Lectures 11–12.

Use Kevin Zhou:

- W1 Waves I
- W2 where Fourier ideas support diffraction

Main goals:

- understand Fourier series as mode decomposition;
- understand wave packets;
- know phase velocity;
- know group velocity;
- understand dispersion qualitatively.

Do not spend too much time on full Fourier-transform or signal-analysis machinery.

---

### Stage 4: Electromagnetic Waves

Study selected parts of MIT Lectures 13–16.

Use Kevin Zhou:

- E7 Electrodynamics

Main goals:

- understand EM plane waves;
- use \(E=cB\);
- compute intensity;
- use the Poynting vector;
- understand radiation pressure;
- understand qualitative scattering.

Skip the engineering-style details of transmission lines, coaxial cables, skin depth, waveguides, and resonant cavities unless doing enrichment.

---

### Stage 5: Interfaces, Polarization, and Geometrical Optics

Study selected parts of MIT Lecture 18.

Use Kevin Zhou:

- W3 Specific Waves
- E8 Materials and Fresnel Equations

Main goals:

- use Snell’s law;
- understand total internal reflection;
- understand Brewster angle;
- understand polarization basics;
- understand Malus’s law;
- connect to basic ray optics.

Skip full Fresnel coefficient algebra unless specifically needed for advanced practice.

---

### Stage 6: Interference and Diffraction

Study MIT Lectures 20–21 carefully.

Use Kevin Zhou:

- W2 Interference and Diffraction

Main goals:

- solve double-slit interference problems;
- solve thin-film interference problems;
- use phase and path difference correctly;
- solve diffraction grating problems;
- solve single-slit diffraction problems;
- understand resolution and Rayleigh criterion.

This is one of the highest-yield sections of MIT 8.03SC.

---

### Stage 7: Optional Applications

Use MIT Lecture 22 only as optional application practice.

Treat MIT Lecture 23 as enrichment.

## 11. Final Recommendation

Use old MIT 8.03SC as a lecture and intuition resource, but use Kevin Zhou’s handouts to define the preparation boundary.

The recommended path is:

1. Study Lectures 1–5 carefully.
2. Study Lecture 6 selectively.
3. Study Lectures 7–9 carefully.
4. Study Lectures 11–12 selectively.
5. Study Lectures 13–16 selectively.
6. Mostly skip Lecture 17.
7. Study Lecture 18 selectively.
8. Study Lectures 20–21 carefully.
9. Use Lecture 22 only for optional applications.
10. Treat Lecture 23 as enrichment.

The essential judgment is:

> **Old MIT 8.03SC is highly useful for the main ideas of Kevin Zhou’s oscillation, wave, EM-wave, and optics handouts, but the course repeatedly goes deeper than necessary. For efficient USAPhO preparation, learn the ideas that match M4, W1, W2, W3, E7, and E8, and skip the specialized undergraduate-wave-physics extensions unless doing enrichment.**
