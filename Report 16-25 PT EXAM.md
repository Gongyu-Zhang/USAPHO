# USAPhO Topic Analysis Report: 2016–2025

## Scope and method

I used the official AAPT “Past Exams” archive and included only the **USAPhO exam** PDFs for **2016–2025**. I excluded all **F = ma / Fnet = ma** exams, solutions-only documents, training camp exams, and USAPhO Plus material. The AAPT archive lists prior exams and solutions as PDFs and, for the requested years, separately lists USAPhO exam links alongside F = ma / Fnet = ma links. ([aapt.org][1])

A note on numbering: **2016 and 2017** used **four Part A questions and two Part B questions**, so those years include A1–A4 and B1–B2; later years in this range are treated as six-problem exams, usually A1–A3 and B1–B3. ([aapt.org][2])

The classifications below are my analytic coding of the **dominant solution path**. Many USAPhO problems are intentionally interdisciplinary, so I list both a **primary domain** for statistics and **secondary topic tags** for study planning. Official problem statements used for coding came from the AAPT PDFs for 2016–2018, 2019–2021, 2022–2024, and 2025. ([aapt.org][2]) ([aapt.org][3]) ([aapt.org][4]) ([aapt.org][5])

## Two-tier topic hierarchy used

| Tier 1 domain                            | Tier 2 subtopics used in this report                                                                                                                              |
| ---------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Mechanics**                            | Kinematics and collisions; statics/friction/elasticity; rotation and angular momentum; oscillations and stability; gravitation/orbits; fluids and surface tension |
| **Electricity & Magnetism**              | Electrostatics/capacitance; magnetostatics; induction and motional emf; circuits and resonance; EM field momentum/energy/radiation; transport                     |
| **Thermodynamics & Statistical Physics** | Heat engines/entropy; ideal and real gases; heat transfer; phase changes; blackbody/radiative thermodynamics; heat capacity/statistical mechanics                 |
| **Waves & Optics**                       | Acoustics/Doppler; geometric optics/refraction; interference/diffraction/resolution; resonators, lasers, Fourier/mode-locking                                     |
| **Relativity & Modern Physics**          | Special relativity kinematics; relativistic energy-momentum; particle physics; quantum/uncertainty; solid-state/condensed-matter models                           |

Astrophysics, data fitting, scaling, and estimation are treated as **cross-cutting contexts** rather than separate primary domains, because most such problems are testing mechanics, thermodynamics, optics, E&M, or relativity through an astrophysical or experimental wrapper.

**Trend interpretation:**

4. **Thermodynamics/stat mech is steady, not occasional.** It appears in almost every year except 2025 by primary classification, and even when absent as a primary domain, thermodynamic ideas can appear as secondary modeling tools.

# 5. Topic trends and study implications

## A. Mechanics

**Frequency:** 17/60 primary problems, highest of all domains.

**Recurring patterns:**

* **Rotation and angular momentum** are extremely common: rolling spheres, dominoes, rotating disks, ants on disks, wet-animal shaking, and constrained rotating systems.
* **Constraints** are a major USAPhO signature: bead-rod-ball systems, frictionless surfaces, disk/ant frames, rolling without slipping, no-slip friction thresholds.
* **Statics and elasticity** appear in wedges, ramps, stick-slip, bending moments, magnetic stress in solenoids, and membranes.
* **Fluid/surface-tension mechanics** is not rare: falling water stream instability, charged droplets, wet fur, and convection-like thermal transport.

**Preparation priority:** Very high. A student should be fluent with Newtonian mechanics at the level of Lagrangian-style reasoning even if not formally using Lagrangians: constraints, generalized coordinates, conservation of energy/momentum/angular momentum, normal forces, stability, and perturbation expansions.

## B. Electricity & Magnetism

**Frequency:** 16/60 primary problems, nearly tied with mechanics.

**Recurring patterns:**

* **Electrostatics appears in many guises:** charged spheres, cubes, rings, droplets, membranes, accelerators, and semiconductor transport.
* **Circuits are not just introductory circuits:** high-gain devices, nonlinear I-V elements, LC radiation, RLC resonance, and data-based Q-factor extraction.
* **Magnetism/induction often couples to mechanics:** motional emf, rotating rods, solenoid pressure, magnetic deflection, EM angular momentum.
* **Field energy/stress is a recurring advanced theme:** electrostatic pressure, magnetic pressure, field angular momentum, classical electron energy.

**Preparation priority:** Very high. Students should go beyond standard AP-level circuit and field problems. They should practice field-energy methods, scaling of capacitance/inductance, motional emf, Maxwell-stress-style pressure, and unusual geometry.

## C. Thermodynamics & Statistical Physics

**Frequency:** 11/60 primary problems.

**Recurring patterns:**

* **Heat engines and entropy** recur: finite reservoirs, Carnot limits, planetary heat engines, phase-change engines, and heating optimization.
* **Real-gas/stat-mech modeling** is common: virial expansion, van der Waals fits, real gas free expansion, Einstein solid heat capacity.
* **Thermal radiation and astrophysical thermodynamics** appear through blackbody stars, radiation pressure, and stellar stability.
* **Phase change and heat transfer** appear in ice growth, latent heat engines, and conduction/convection estimates.

**Preparation priority:** High. Students should know the first and second laws deeply, not just formulas. Entropy accounting, reversible/irreversible limits, Carnot bounds, real gas corrections, and scaling estimates are especially important.

## D. Waves & Optics

**Frequency:** 7/60 primary problems, but growing in recent years.

**Recurring patterns:**

* **Geometric optics** appears through rainbows/caustics, lenses, magnification, and distortion.
* **Wave optics** appears through interference, diffraction, resolution, Fabry-Perot resonators, and mode locking.
* **Acoustics** appears through Doppler analysis and sonic-boom geometry.
* **Photon/statistical effects** appear in telescope resolution, shot noise, and uncertainty-limited optics.

**Preparation priority:** Medium-high. The raw frequency is lower than mechanics/E&M, but modern USAPhO optics problems can be very hard. Students should practice deriving results from wave phase, path difference, Fourier sums, small-angle approximations, and ray geometry.

## E. Relativity & Modern Physics

**Frequency:** 9/60 primary problems, with a clear increase in 2021–2024.

**Recurring patterns:**

* **Special relativity** appears repeatedly: world-line diagrams, velocity addition, Doppler, proper time, simultaneity.
* **Relativistic energy-momentum** appears in particle thresholds, muon collider estimates, light sails, and cosmic/astrophysical particles.
* **Quantum and solid-state models** appear through uncertainty, electron shells, white dwarfs, band dispersion, Einstein solids, and mode locking.
* **Particle astrophysics** appears through cosmic rays, neutrinos, and supernova timing.

**Preparation priority:** High for top scores. Relativity/modern problems may be fewer than mechanics/E&M, but they are often high-discrimination problems. Students should be comfortable with Lorentz factors, energy-momentum relations, relativistic Doppler, time dilation, and order-of-magnitude quantum estimates.

# 6. Recommended study weighting

A frequency-based preparation allocation would look like this:

| Study block           | Suggested share | Rationale                                                                      |
| --------------------- | --------------: | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Mechanics**         |          25–30% | Most frequent; foundational for many mixed-domain problems                     |
| **E&M**               |          25–30% | Nearly as frequent as mechanics; often appears in advanced, nonstandard setups |
| **Thermo/stat mech**  |          15–20% | Very steady; common in modeling, optimization, and astrophysical contexts      |
| **Relativity/modern** |          15–20% | Increasingly important; high-discrimination topic                              |
| **Waves/optics**      |          10–15% | Lower count but increasingly sophisticated and often mathematically demanding  |

# 8. Strategic conclusions for USAPhO preparation

## Highest-yield strategy

The best preparation sequence is:

1. **Master mechanics and E&M first.** Together they form the majority of the exam and also supply tools for mixed-domain problems.
2. **Then add thermo/stat and relativity/modern.** These are frequent enough that skipping them is costly, and recent exams have emphasized modern/relativistic reasoning.
3. **Treat optics/waves as a precision topic.** It appears less often but can be conceptually dense, especially when combined with diffraction, Fourier sums, or uncertainty.
4. **Practice mixed modeling.** USAPhO problems often start from unfamiliar physical situations and reward building a model from first principles.
5. **Train approximations deliberately.** Small-angle approximations, Taylor expansions, dimensional analysis, leading-order scaling, and order-of-magnitude estimates appear throughout the decade.

## What distinguishes USAPhO from easier contest physics

The exam is less about recognizing a standard formula and more about building a model. Across 2016–2025, the recurring advanced skills are:

* translating a physical story into variables and assumptions;
* deciding which effects are negligible;
* deriving scaling laws;
* using conservation laws under constraints;
* linearizing around equilibrium;
* connecting energy, force, and stability;
* interpreting or fitting data/graphs;
* combining multiple physics domains in one solution.

## Final study priority ranking

For a student preparing under time pressure:

1. **Mechanics: rotation, constraints, stability, fluids/surface tension**
2. **E&M: electrostatics, circuits, induction, magnetic/field energy**
3. **Thermo/stat: entropy, heat engines, real gases, radiation, phase change**
4. **Relativity/modern: SR kinematics, energy-momentum, quantum estimates**
5. **Waves/optics: interference, diffraction, geometric optics, acoustics**

The most efficient preparation is not to chase every exotic context, but to become very strong at the reusable physics tools that appear underneath those contexts.
