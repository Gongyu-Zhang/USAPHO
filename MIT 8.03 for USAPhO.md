## What Kevin Zhou actually covers

### M4 Oscillations

Kevin’s **M4 Oscillations** directly supports MIT 8.03 Lectures 1–6. It covers small oscillations, Taylor expansion near stable equilibrium, damped oscillations, driven oscillations, transients, steady-state response, amplitude, phase, and normal modes. 

So for MIT 8.03:

* **L1 Periodic Phenomena** — fully relevant.
* **L2 Damped Free Oscillations** — fully relevant.
* **L3–L4 Forced Oscillations, resonance, transients** — relevant.
* **L5 Coupled Oscillators** — relevant.
* **L6 Driven Coupled Oscillators** — partially relevant; the physical idea is useful, but long algebraic forced-coupled-oscillator calculations are lower priority.

The important boundary is that Kevin’s M4 also includes advanced material, such as Lagrangian/energy treatments of more complex oscillators and adiabatic invariants. He explicitly notes that arbitrary many-degree-of-freedom small-oscillation problems can become intractable and generally require a computer, and the adiabatic-invariant discussion points toward Hamiltonian mechanics beyond the usual olympiad syllabus. 

**Updated call:** MIT oscillations are mostly in-scope, but do not overtrain arbitrary large coupled systems, Hamiltonian formalism, or advanced adiabatic-invariant methods.

## W1 Waves I

Kevin’s **W1 Waves I** is much broader than just “string waves.” It covers the wave equation, waves on a string, traveling-wave solutions, standing waves, musical harmonics, reflection, transmission, impedance, Fourier series, wave packets, phase velocity, and group velocity. 

This means that for MIT 8.03:

* **L7 Many Coupled Oscillators & Wave Equations** — relevant, especially the passage from many oscillators to waves.
* **L8 Traveling & Standing Waves** — fully relevant.
* **L9 Normal Modes in Sound and Music** — relevant.
* **L11 Fourier Analysis** — Fourier **series** is relevant; full Fourier-transform/spectral analysis is much less important.
* **L12 Dispersion, Phase Velocity, Group Velocity** — more relevant than I previously implied.

However, W1 also contains advanced or enrichment-level material: arbitrary-dimensional wave equations using vector calculus, plate vibrations governed by a biharmonic equation, wave-packet uncertainty ideas, and toy field-theory/Higgs-style examples. 

**Updated call:** phase velocity, group velocity, wave packets, and Fourier series are **not beyond Kevin-style USAPhO prep**. But full Fourier transforms, spectra, arbitrary-dimensional PDE methods, and plate theory are beyond efficient USAPhO prep.

## W2 Interference and Diffraction

Kevin’s **W2** is very aligned with the later optics part of MIT 8.03. It covers double-slit interference, coherence, Huygens principle, far-field approximations, image-source reasoning, Fermat/Huygens-style ideas, thin films, diffraction gratings, single-slit diffraction, Airy disks, higher-dimensional diffraction, crystallography, and diffraction-limited resolution. 

So for MIT 8.03:

* **L20 Interference** — very high priority.
* **L21 Diffraction** — very high priority.
* Parts of **L22 Rainbows and Other Optical Phenomena** — useful only as geometric-optics practice, not as required content.

The subtlety is that W2 itself goes beyond the usual USAPhO core in places. Kevin includes higher-dimensional diffraction, DNA/crystallography-style examples, and real-world telescope-array applications. Those are good enrichment, but not essential unless you are aiming beyond USAPhO toward IPhO-level depth. 

**Updated call:** MIT interference and diffraction should be studied carefully. The extra real-world diffraction applications are optional.

## E7 Electrodynamics

This is the biggest reason to revise the previous answer. Kevin’s recommended core path includes **E7**, and E7 covers EM waves, displacement current, field energy, Poynting vector, radiation pressure, Larmor radiation, and Rayleigh/Thomson scattering. 

Kevin’s E7 gives the standard EM-wave facts: (E), (B), and propagation direction are mutually perpendicular; (E_0=cB_0); and (c=1/\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}). It also discusses EM-wave energy, momentum, radiation pressure, and standing EM waves. 

So for MIT 8.03:

* **L13 Deriving Electromagnetic Waves** — relevant, but study the result and basic derivation, not every vector-calculus detail.
* **L14 Generating EM Waves, Energy, Scattering** — more relevant than I previously implied. Poynting vector, radiation pressure, and qualitative Rayleigh scattering are fair game in Kevin-style prep.
* **L16 Electromagnetic Waves and Conductors** — partly relevant, especially boundary/reflection/radiation-pressure ideas; skin depth and transmission-line details are much lower priority.
* **L17 Wave Guides & Resonant Cavities** — still mostly beyond.

Kevin does include Larmor radiation and scattering, but those should be treated as **conceptual/estimate-level** material for USAPhO, not as something requiring full antenna-theory mastery. 

**Updated call:** EM-wave energy, momentum, Poynting vector, radiation pressure, and qualitative scattering are in-scope. Antenna radiation, skin depth, waveguides, cavities, and transmission-line cutoff are mostly beyond USAPhO-efficient prep.

## W3 Specific Waves

Kevin’s **W3** is important because it covers many things that MIT 8.03 also covers: sound waves, Doppler effect, polarization, water waves, reflection/refraction, Snell’s law, mirages, ray tracing, pinhole cameras, lenses, and geometrical optics. 

But W3 is **not** in Kevin’s recommended 24-handout core path for USAPhO; his syllabus says the core path includes W1 and W2, while the other units are more relevant for IPhO preparation. 

So W3 topics should be treated as **selective but real olympiad material**, not as irrelevant.

For MIT 8.03, this means:

* **L9 Sound and music** — relevant.
* **L15 Doppler Effect** — relevant, especially sound Doppler and simple wave-Doppler reasoning.
* **L18 Interactions of Light with Nonconductors** — relevant for Snell’s law, total internal reflection, Brewster angle, and basic polarization.
* **L22 Rainbows and Other Optical Phenomena** — useful as practice, but specialized.

Kevin’s W3 also goes into complex polarization, circular/elliptical polarization, birefringence, quarter-wave plates, half-wave plates, Faraday rotators, water waves, and detailed geometrical-optics devices. 

**Updated call:** basic sound, Doppler, polarization, Snell’s law, TIR, lenses, and ray tracing are in-scope. Wave plates, Faraday rotators, detailed birefringence, and detailed water-wave theory are lower priority for USAPhO.

## E8 Materials and Fresnel equations

Kevin’s **E8** includes EM waves at dielectric interfaces, boundary conditions, reflection/refraction laws, Snell’s law, Fresnel equations, total internal reflection with evanescent transmitted waves, and frequency-dependent permittivity/dispersion. 

This overlaps with MIT 8.03 **L18 Interactions of Light with Nonconductors**.

But E8 is **not** in the recommended core USAPhO path; it is more of a synthesis/tricky-materials handout. So the right USAPhO boundary is:

* Know **Snell’s law**.
* Know **total internal reflection**.
* Know **Brewster angle** qualitatively and perhaps the simple formula.
* Understand that reflection/transmission depend on polarization.
* Do **not** spend too much time deriving or memorizing full Fresnel amplitude/intensity formulas unless you are aiming for IPhO-level depth.

## Best practical study plan from MIT 8.03 using Kevin Zhou’s coverage

For **USAPhO with Kevin Zhou as the guide**, I would study MIT 8.03 in this order:

1. **Do Lectures 1–5 carefully.**
   These map almost directly to M4: SHM, damping, driving, resonance, coupled oscillators, and normal modes.

2. **Do Lectures 7–9 carefully.**
   These map to W1: wave equation, traveling waves, standing waves, sound, harmonics, and normal modes.

3. **Do selected Lecture 11.**
   Learn Fourier series for normal modes. Do not overinvest in Fourier transforms, spectra, or signal analysis.

4. **Do selected Lecture 12.**
   Learn phase velocity, group velocity, dispersion, and wave-packet intuition.

5. **Do selected Lectures 13–16.**
   Learn EM waves, (E=cB), Poynting vector, intensity, radiation pressure, qualitative scattering, Doppler, and basic conductor reflection. Skip transmission-line and waveguide-level detail.

6. **Mostly skip Lecture 17.**

7. **Do selected Lecture 18.**
   Learn Snell’s law, total internal reflection, Brewster angle, and basic polarization. Treat full Fresnel equations as optional.

8. **Do Lectures 20–21 carefully.**
   These are central: interference, thin films, diffraction gratings, single-slit diffraction, and resolution.

9. **Use Lecture 22 only as application practice.**

10. **Treat Lecture 23 as enrichment.**
